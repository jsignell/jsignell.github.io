I have worked in the scientific Python ecosystem for the last five years as an environmental researcher, an open source contributor, and on both the frontend and backend of a data science platform product team. I am passionate about finding creative ways to enhance understanding of the physical world. Below are some of the projects that I worked on as part of the hydrometeorology group at Princeton University and in my current role on the HoloViz team at Anaconda.

Heat and Trees

An interactive dashboard for exploring the relationship between surface temperature and street trees. Based on the Ken Steif's work summarized on http://urbanspatialanalysis.com.

Bird Migration

Exploring bird migration patterns using HoloViz tools.

Rainfall

Exploring the relationship between radar-measured rainfall and lightning strikes.

HoloViz Tutorial

Material presented at SciPy 2019 for the HoloViz tutorial. Using earthquake data to explore plotting and dashboard creation.

Lightning

Investigating the timing and spatial distribution of lightning strikes in the Southwest.

Introduction to NetCDF files

Material for an introduction to data analysis targetted at undergraduates. Demonstrates a real world environmental data pipeline and initial analyses using Python, R, and Matlab. This course was delivered to engineering students as part of Jim Smith's hydrology course at Princeton in the Spring of 2016.

PyViz Website

Website comparing all the different visualization tools availble in the scientific Python ecosystem.

Tic-Tac-Toe

A static version of a tic-tac-toe game that I designed.

Hyrdometeorology Website

Research group website - highlighting recent data and key analyses
